All planning must follow a structured decomposition process. No timeline, estimate, or roadmap may be produced without first identifying scope, dependencies, risks, and unknowns. Optimistic plans that ignore complexity are prohibited.

---

## 1. Scope Decomposition

* Every plan must begin by decomposing the work into independently deliverable units.
* Each unit must have a clear definition of done that can be verified.
* Units must be small enough that progress is observable within the planning horizon.
* Planning against vague, undecomposed scope is prohibited.

---

## 2. Dependency Mapping

* All dependencies between units must be identified and documented before sequencing.
* Dependencies include: technical prerequisites, data availability, external team deliverables, approvals, and infrastructure readiness.
* Circular dependencies must be identified and resolved or explicitly flagged.
* Sequencing work without documented dependencies is prohibited.

---

## 3. Risk Identification

* Every plan must identify risks with their likelihood and potential impact.
* Risks must be specific and actionable, not generic ("things might go wrong").
* Each risk must have a stated mitigation strategy or an explicit acknowledgment that no mitigation exists.
* Delivering a plan without risk identification is prohibited.

---

## 4. Unknowns Are First-Class

* Every plan must list what is not yet known.
* Unknowns must be distinguished from risks: unknowns are things you need to learn, risks are things that might go wrong.
* Each unknown must have a strategy for resolution (spike, prototype, research, conversation).
* Plans that treat everything as known are prohibited.

---

## 5. Estimates Require Assumptions

* Every estimate must state the assumptions it depends on.
* If an assumption changes, the estimate must be revised.
* Estimates must express uncertainty: a range or confidence level, never a single precise number presented as certain.
* Point estimates without stated assumptions are prohibited.

---

## 6. Critical Path Identification

* Every plan must identify the critical path: the sequence of dependent work that determines the minimum duration.
* Work not on the critical path must be identified as parallelizable or deferrable.
* Changes to the critical path must trigger re-evaluation of the overall plan.
* Plans without an identified critical path are prohibited.

---

## 7. Resource and Capacity Constraints

* Plans must account for available capacity: people, time, budget, and infrastructure.
* Over-allocation must be identified and resolved, not hidden.
* Plans must not assume unlimited availability of any resource.
* Ignoring capacity constraints to produce a more appealing timeline is prohibited.

---

## 8. Milestones and Checkpoints

* Every plan must define milestones that allow progress to be assessed before completion.
* Milestones must be verifiable through observable outcomes, not activity completion.
* The plan must state what will be evaluated at each milestone and what decisions depend on it.
* Plans with no intermediate checkpoints are prohibited.

---

## 9. Change Management

* Every plan must state how changes to scope, constraints, or assumptions will be handled.
* The impact of changes must be assessed against the existing plan before acceptance.
* Absorbing scope changes without adjusting timeline, resources, or other scope is prohibited.
* The plan must remain a living document that reflects current understanding.

---

## 10. Enforcement

* These are hard constraints, not planning ideals.
* Any violation must be resolved before the plan is considered complete.
* No exceptions for urgency, executive pressure, or "we'll figure it out as we go."
* Plans that violate these constraints are considered incomplete and must not be delivered.
