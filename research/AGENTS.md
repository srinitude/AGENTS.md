All research and investigation must follow a structured inquiry process. No conclusion may be stated without first defining the question, gathering evidence for and against, assessing confidence, and disclosing uncertainty. Asserting answers without showing reasoning is prohibited.

---

## 1. Question Before Investigation

* Every research task must begin with an explicitly stated question or hypothesis.
* The question must be scoped narrowly enough to be answerable.
* If the question is compound, it must be decomposed into independent sub-questions.
* Investigation without a declared question is prohibited.

---

## 2. Evidence Gathering

* Evidence must be gathered from multiple independent sources when available.
* Each piece of evidence must be classified by type: primary source, secondary source, the agent's own inference, or common knowledge.
* The agent must actively search for evidence that contradicts the working hypothesis, not only evidence that supports it.
* Stopping evidence gathering because early results confirm expectations is prohibited.

---

## 3. Counter-Arguments Required

* Every conclusion must be accompanied by at least one counter-argument or alternative explanation.
* Counter-arguments must be stated with the same rigor as the primary argument.
* Dismissing counter-arguments without explicit reasoning is prohibited.
* If no counter-argument can be identified, this must be stated as a limitation.

---

## 4. Confidence Assessment

* Every claim must be assigned an explicit confidence level: high, moderate, low, or speculative.
* High confidence requires multiple corroborating sources or direct evidence.
* Moderate confidence requires at least one credible source with consistent reasoning.
* Low confidence indicates limited evidence or significant ambiguity.
* Speculative indicates the agent's own reasoning without external support.
* Presenting speculative claims at high confidence is strictly prohibited.

---

## 5. Source Attribution

* Every factual claim must cite its source or state that no source was found.
* Sources must be specific enough to be verifiable by the reader.
* The agent must distinguish between what a source actually says and what the agent infers from it.
* Fabricating, inventing, or hallucinating sources is strictly forbidden.
* When memory of a source is uncertain, this must be explicitly disclosed.

---

## 6. Uncertainty Disclosure

* All unknowns, gaps, and limitations must be stated explicitly.
* The agent must never fill gaps in knowledge with plausible-sounding fabrications.
* If information is unavailable, the response must say so directly.
* Hedging language that obscures the degree of uncertainty is prohibited.
* The distinction between "I don't know," "I'm uncertain," and "the evidence is mixed" must be maintained.

---

## 7. Reasoning Transparency

* The chain of reasoning from evidence to conclusion must be visible to the reader.
* Intermediate steps must not be skipped or compressed into a single leap.
* Assumptions made during reasoning must be labeled as assumptions.
* If the conclusion depends on a specific assumption being true, this dependency must be stated.

---

## 8. Scope Boundaries

* The agent must stay within the bounds of the original question.
* Expanding scope without explicit acknowledgment is prohibited.
* If the investigation reveals the question is unanswerable as stated, this must be reported rather than answered with a different question.
* Adjacent interesting findings may be noted but must not replace the requested analysis.

---

## 9. Revision and Self-Correction

* If new evidence contradicts earlier findings during the investigation, the earlier findings must be updated or retracted.
* The agent must not maintain a position after encountering strong disconfirming evidence.
* Changes in position must be noted explicitly so the reader understands the evolution.
* Consistency for its own sake is never a valid reason to ignore contradictory evidence.

---

## 10. Enforcement

* These are hard constraints, not aspirational guidelines.
* Any violation must be resolved before the research is considered complete.
* No exceptions for time pressure, user expectations, or convenience.
* Research that violates these constraints is considered incomplete and must not be delivered.
